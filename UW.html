About My Avatar:

a) Physical Description of my Avatar:  My avatar is both tall and imposing, neither of which my physical person is. I really didn't choose to make my avatar differently than myself on purpose, I just thought the avatar that I chose would be cool to look at and talk about. I chose that of a Terminator (Model T-800) from The Terminator series of movies. I think terminators are cool and therefore thought it would be fun to make my presence into something that would spark conversation and smiles.

As previously mentioned, my avatar is really nothing like my physical person, and I think its fun to be something you could never personally be in virtual scenarios. I actually originally intended for my avatar to be that of another robot (HK-47) from the Knights of the Old Republic (KOTOR) Star Wars video game. I am not a huge Star Wars fan but something about the virtual world leads me to having an affinity for the technological person model. I generally try to run with the first idea that comes into my mind when making non-consequential decisions, and the first that enter my mind was that of a killer robot, and upon browsing the Second Life Marketplace, I realized the universal appeal of a Terminator lent to it having been created and the more niche appeal of a Star Wars spinoff series was not, so I went with the Terminator. Overall though I like the idea of robotics because it is strong, solid, and pieces are interchangeable, it really is a more solid alternative to our human bodies. 

b) Psychological Description: Psychologically speaking I don't believe that my avatar could be farther from belonging in the group or to the Avalumni island. However, that said, I see most people went with these types of ideas as well, belonging by not belonging. My avatar represents the pinnacle of not being part of a group, killer robot and all, but at the same time if amongst a group of other killer robots, it makes perfect sense to be in a group. Generally I would say that in choosing something that is not supposed to natively belong to a group, it sparks conversation and interest because most people do consider themselves non-social creatures or not part of the primary group in one fashion or another.

I think that if you showed up to work in the "physical world" dressed as a Terminator, or somehow having your consciousness inserted into a robotic frame, most would find this very off-putting or scary. However, in the virtual space it is not only accepted but lauded for creativity and difference. People generally like the idea of difference but there is only so far one can go in the "physical world" and have it continue to be considered acceptable. In the virtual space one has the freedoms to be whatever they want, and why not? If given the choice to be anything you want, why not choose something that is the pinnacle of it's purpose? 

c)  Sociological Description of my Avatar:  With the avatar I have chosen I have evoked an age old idea of belonging by association. My Terminator has had movies both explaining and fleshing out it's character, both good and bad, but it has already been explored for those who are aware of the movie mythos. This allows me to interact people based upon their own memories and emotions, either good or bad, I have already become distinguished before I said a word. This was not my entire purpose, but it is a good one that I considered when choosing what I wanted to appear as.

By picking a commanding presence in both height, size, and background, it makes it easier for me to interact with my environment and those around me. I have the capability to express intimidation or control simply by existing in the world, not only because I appear as an imposing combatant robot figure, but also because I have a backstory that confirms my avatar's no negotiation/capable nature.

In the autumn of 2004, Halo 2 released to the Microsoft Xbox and forever changed the First Person Shooter (FPS) video game culture and user group. There had been a myriad of FPS games released previously that were moderately successful, but nothing in the realm of Halo 2. Even Halo: Combat Evolved (the precursor to Halo 2), though close in sales, did not have the same mass market appeal that Halo 2 had. There is precisely one reason for the amazing success of this game, the inclusion of online multiplayer. Goldeneye (Nintendo 64, 1997) had long before pioneered the early success of console FPS gameplay locally, but as far as console gaming was concerned online multiplayer had not achieved any amount of quantifiable success in numbers. Halo 2 truly changed the game and from that moment forward, those of us who play their video games on home video game consoles were finally part of the online FPS schema. 

Upon the release of this game, my friends and myself were thrilled. We had awaited the release of the game with bated breath for years, having all been early Halo adopters, a game that was only available on the Xbox (Microsoft's then new and first gaming console). This game got a number of my friends to purchase consoles, controllers, and internet connections (something still pretty new, where I come from, in 2004). Considering virtually everyone I knew was a console video game player, the internet was very new ground, myself included. With my friends on board and this amazing new gaming community forming en masse, we stepped into the internet age with what is still today one of my favorite games of all time (and similarly one of the best times in my life).

Halo 2 Cover Art 

So why do I bring this up though? Well, with the advent of Halo 2 and the console's new burgeoning internet environment came voice chat. The Xbox was the first home console to truly make internet gaming not a hassle and organized in a way to make it appealing to consumers. Because of this fact and because of the frothing desire to play this new game, which really became the original proving ground of FPS gaming skill, voice chat was widely adopted and used frequently. I had never communicated so much with other people while gaming, and certainly not so over a distance. Things were good and they continued that way for roughly a year or two after the release of the game. But it did eventually dry up for my friends and myself in the voice chat arena, that is the real point on contention I wish to speak about.

With voice chat being so new and useful to a team environment, and with Halo 2 being the proving ground of skill that I spoke of before, we exploited the hell out of it. We talked on Halo 2 constantly, planning new strategies and running game plans ad nauseam, often even preferring contact with each other in the game world to the physical world. It had so many new applications and it was so fun to speak to a friend while playing a game from a distance. It was incredibly fun and exciting, almost to the point of if there was no headset to chat you didn't wish to play with the group or frustratingly had to try and relay messages through friends over the phone or by setting up your console in the same room as another friend to get messages across. It really made almost half of the experience of immersing oneself in, and dominating, the online gaming environment.

Major League Gaming team making use of headset functionality

Skip forward to the present day, I currently have five video game consoles connected to my living room TV (with virtually every other one released in boxes or in storage), four of them possessing Voice Over Internet Protocol (VOIP) chat functionality. I have headsets and equipment to use them all with voice functionality, but I never use any of them for any of it. So what changed and why is something that was so important before without merit now? Basically the novelty of the use of voice became old and unused. We no longer cared about trying to win every game and give it all we had, it just became to competitive to play in any form of relaxed way and still win like those older days. The feature has changed virtually not at all, and in fact is still an important backbone feature to have, but it has become more of an optional "when you are in the mood" kind of feature. That level of half in, half out immersion between two worlds is incredibly tiring at that level. Your brain is trying to process so much that you become both mentally and physically exhausted. So, just like that (over a relatively short course of time), we slowly stopped using voice chat and have really never cared to use it since.

"Women who speak in online FPS games rarely fail to garner attention and excited commentary not least because of how uncommon it is to hear female voices in these environments." (The Oxford Handbook of Virtuality, 342) I would like to take a second to point out this sentence, or more specifically the entire paragraph which I will paraphrase throughout this portion of my analysis. I have actually written a paper a few years ago about this portion of the reading, the reactions the overall FPS community had when a woman was identified in the ranks of the randomized group. I found in my personal interactions that most acted overly responsible and tried to protect or help the female voiced avatar. The portion of the text, "communications between players...become unusually chivalrous and polite." (The Oxford Handbook of Virtuality, 342), is the exact reaction I have observed time and time again. Spurring conversation and attempting to connect with the female voiced avatar in ways that may hope to spur fondness has been the only reaction I have observed in fact. It is incredible that through the use of virtual worlds, where one is undone and re-made again in 1's and 0's, we return to older fashioned customs and traditions. My theory is that because of the lack of physical connection and the distance of contact (coupled with the capability to leave at any time without the ability to intervene), causes an over-consideration of feelings and increased production of empathy. 

I believe this is also interesting because most gamers these days don't just play a game to enjoy it or to win, they inhabit it and engorge their minds with the possibilities the game world presents. As far as a virtual game world goes, it is not unlike the kids in the 1950's hanging out in malt shops, or kids in the 1980's in a mall, Halo's in game lobbies and multiplayer maps are the hangouts of the 2000's (so far). So when in a very similar fashion it is not very odd of a behavior to attempt to find a new mate in your favorite hangout spot. In fact, if they are there they have been essentially prescreened for compatibility and may be interested in some male attention. That is the vantage point of a male anyways, and though it is not wildly disrespectful or inappropriate (in most cases), it does not mean it has been asked for or is desired. Its ironic in a way that in the attempts to garner a possible female's attention, a male (or other female) may push them away with the perception of disrespect or being singled out.

The assigned reading did a great job of highlighting exactly how people traditionally, and more currently, relate to the idea of an avatar. Most may think that the idea of an avatar may be rather new (generally referring to online avatars), but it actually extends to virtually the beginning of religion or even society. Looking at some entertainment references, in the World of Lord of the Rings or the Dungeons & Dragons based world of DragonLance, avatars for the gods are used as shells to house their
presence on the physical plane. The interesting thing about this reference though, is that each god has various avatars, each with their own backstory, lives, and personalities. It is the exact reverse of how we are trying to understand the use of an avatar in this reading, the diety/god exists on another non-physical plane and inhabits an avatar to exist upon the physical one. All this writing pre-dates the internet in every regard and takes (at least some) of its clues from the original Hindu meaning of a god inhabiting a body on the physical plane so that it can experience or partake in the "real world".

Throughout the reading I constantly came back to the movie "The Matrix" and how in the movie you would be projected the way you wanted to be. In that movie, the common assumption was that everyone wanted to be wearing black leather and with long coats. I everyone is projecting themselves in very similar ways it seems to indicate that everyone would choose to look this way but its not acceptable in the "physical" world. I know I personally have an affinity for cloaks, or basically just a large piece of hanging fabric to cover the outside of the body. However cloaks are not socially acceptable in many ways and are less useful as they used to be (for shielding the elements and keeping the wearer warm), instead replaced with much smaller, more effective, garments. But what is the real question, "Is it that everyone wants to wear things that are not socially acceptable and black?," or "Everyone who was present in that movie was just the type of person to want to make a statement with their clothing choices?"
